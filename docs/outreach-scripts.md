## COMPETITIVE POSITIONING CHEAT SHEET

Use these talking points when the conversation gets comparative:

### vs. Fullscript
- Fullscript = **dispensary** (they make money when clinicians sell supplements)
- PharmaGuide = **transparency tool** (we score what patients already have)
- Fullscript doesn't score bioavailability by form
- Fullscript doesn't detect proprietary blend disclosure levels
- Fullscript doesn't flag harmful additives with severity + regulatory jurisdiction
- **Blindside:** Fullscript has financial incentive to recommend products, not flag problems with them

### vs. SuppCo
- Raised $4M+ to build a supplement comparison tool
- Doesn't score by chemical form (the single most important quality signal)
- Doesn't detect blend disclosure levels
- Doesn't cross-reference banned substance databases with analog matching
- **Our edge:** We do more with 33 databases and solo engineering than they do with millions in funding

### vs. Examine.com
- Excellent for research, terrible for product-level analysis
- They tell you "Vitamin D is good for bone health"
- We tell you "THIS product's Vitamin D is ergocalciferol (D2), dosed at 400 IU (20% RDA — suboptimal), in a tablet with 3 harmful additives"
- **Blindside:** Ingredient-level knowledge ≠ product-level intelligence

### vs. Labdoor / ConsumerLab
- Third-party testing is valuable but limited to products they choose to test
- They test maybe hundreds of products. We score 130,000+
- They don't check interactions, don't score by form, don't detect blend deception
- **Our edge:** Systematic coverage vs. selective sampling

### Industry Numbers That Hit Hard
- **$60B+** — US supplement market size (and growing)
- **~80%** of Americans take at least one supplement
- The FDA doesn't require supplements to prove they work BEFORE going to market (DSHEA 1994)
- Proprietary blends are **legal** — companies can hide individual ingredient amounts
- Multiple supplement companies have been caught with banned substances (SARMs, DMAA, DNP) in products sold on Amazon
- The supplement industry has less regulatory oversight than the pet food industry
- Most "supplement apps" are affiliate-driven — they make money recommending products, not analyzing them
